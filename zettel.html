about

what this is

The Zettelkasten, literally box of notes, was developed by the sociologist Niklas Luhmann across a career that produced more than 70 books and 400 papers. He worked with roughly 90,000 index cards, each holding one idea, each assigned a short alphanumeric address. Notes did not file under topics. They linked to other notes by reference. The structure that emerged was lateral and associative, capable of generating connections Luhmann himself had not planned.

He described the system as a conversation partner, something that could surprise him, that held more than he had consciously put in. Placing a note next to others gives it a position, and that position generates meaning the note does not carry on its own. The system does not organise knowledge. It multiplies it. A filing system asks where something belongs. The Zettelkasten asks what it touches.

why here

το μανταρίνι moves between theory and lived experience, between the critical and the personal, between registers that rarely share a page. A fragment on attention sits beside a reflection on insomnia. A question about legibility extends a short essay on the body. These proximities are the point.

The Zettelkasten gives that movement a visible structure. Every published entry is a node. Every response is an edge. When one writer responds to another, their entries connect and that connection persists, traversable by anyone who comes after. The archive grows denser with use. Entries with many responses become hubs, centres of gravity where thinking gathers.

Two kinds of connection exist here. A "response" is deliberate: someone chose to engage with something specific. A "see also" connection is structural: entries sharing two or more keywords are linked automatically by the system. Both kinds are visible in the web view.

how to use it

Each entry on the main site carries a short ID at the bottom of its panel, something like m001. Click it to copy. To respond to an entry, click "respond ↗" and the submission form opens with that ID already filled in. When published, your entry connects to it in the network.

A response can be many things: a disagreement, a parallel observation from a different field, something the original entry called to mind, a question it left open. Chains form over time. Branches off an entry use suffixed IDs like "m001a" or "m001b" and appear indented under their root in the transcript view.

the views

Transcript shows entries in reading order, with connections visible inline. Response links appear above the entry text. See also connections appear below. Branch entries are indented under their parent.

Web shows the full archive as a graph. Each circle is an entry, titled inside it. Solid amber lines are "response" connections. Dashed grey lines are "see also" connections. Circles with more connections appear larger. Click any circle to read the entry. Drag circles to rearrange. Pinch or scroll to zoom.

Index lists all keywords alphabetically, each with the entries carrying that keyword. A way into the network by theme.
